Hackerspaces

Hackerspaces are community operated workspaces where people can meet and exchange knowledge mainly about computer hardware and software in the form of peer-to-peer learning, workshops and lectures.

c-base in Berlin

Makerspaces

Sometimes one can not tell the difference between a hackerspace and a makerspace, but if it features a 3D printer, laser cutter and a CNC milling machine, the term makerspace could be more appropriate.

http://www.pbled.org

Men's sheds

Community operated workplaces in Australia for working-class men with an intention to make men and their family lives better.

http://www.forestmensshed.org.au

TechShops

Commercial makerspaces with power tools for advanced making. A tech shop includes a welding station, wood workshop, plastics lab, along with other tools available at other makerspaces.

http://www.techshop.ws

Starting a Fab Lab

10 Things

Massimo Menichinelli, who previously teached this course, created a list of 10 things to consider when starting a fab lab.

http://www.openp2pdesign.org/2013/spaces/10-things-to-do-when-starting-a-fablab

01. Local Conditions

There are different rules in different places. In one country there are no strict regulations regarding indoor machine use, in another it is almost impossible without spending a lot of money.

02. Think About the Community

Is there a local community that could be interested into using a Fab Lab or the community has to be build from scratch?

03. Service and Business Model First

Before you start spending money on machines, design the ways how people would work in the space. How the space would profit?

04. Hidden Costs

Machines are not the only thing that costs money. Consider that you will have to pay for rent, electricity, tables, chairs and at least 1â€“3 people running the space.

05. Allocate Enough Time

There is always not enough time. Allocate extra time for checking and re-checking if everything works. Machine setup might take more time than you think.

06. Balance Team and Resources

Keep the amount of meetings and time for decision making low. 3â€“4 people is considered a decent amount of people to do the job.

07. Careful Choice of Tools

Follow the Fab Lab Inventory maintained by MIT. In case of new machines that are not in the list, consult with other Fab Labs around the globe.

08. It Does not Stop With the Machines

One has to learn how to use them and develop a strategy how to teach that knowledge to users of the lab.

09. Be a Part of the Network

Don't be a lone wolf, visit other labs. If you are doing things alone, it might be that you don't have much time left for communal activities.

10. Test Before Opening

If you have tested the machines already, test again before the inauguration. Developing test projects can be a good way to showcase what can be done in the lab.

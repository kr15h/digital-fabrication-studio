Starting a Fab Lab

10 Things

Massimo Menichinelli, who previously teached this course, created a list of 10 things to consider when starting a fab lab.

http://www.openp2pdesign.org/2013/spaces/10-things-to-do-when-starting-a-fablab

01. Local Conditions

There are different rules in different places. In one country there are no strict regulations regarding indoor machine use, in another it is almost impossible without spending a lot of money.

02. Think About the Community

Is there a local community that could be interested into using a Fab Lab or the community has to be build from scratch?

03. Service and Business Model First

Before you start spending money on machines, design the ways how people would work in the space. How the space would profit?

04. Hidden Costs

Machines are not the only thing that costs money. Consider that you will have to pay for rent, electricity, tables, chairs and at least 1–3 people running the space.

05. Allocate Enough Time

There is always not enough time. Allocate extra time for checking and re-checking if everything works. Machine setup might take more time than you think.

06. Balance Team and Resources

Keep the amount of meetings and time for decision making low. 3–4 people is considered a decent amount of people to do the job.

07. Careful Choice of Tools

Follow the Fab Lab Inventory maintained by MIT. In case of new machines that are not in the list, consult with other Fab Labs around the globe.

08. It Does not Stop With the Machines

One has to learn how to use them and develop a strategy how to teach that knowledge to users of the lab.

09. Be a Part of the Network

Don't be a lone wolf, visit other labs. If you are doing things alone, it might be that you don't have much time left for communal activities.

10. Test Before Opening

If you have tested the machines already, test again before the inauguration. Developing test projects can be a good way to showcase what can be done in the lab.

Business Models

Business Model

"A hypothesis about what customers want, how they want it, and how an enterprise can organize to best meet those needs, get paid for doing so, and make profit."
—http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S002463010900051X

Business Plan

A detailed plan built upon a business model, explains the steps needed to achieve goals stated in the business model. It can have many phases.

Business Model Factors

  • A group of people (customers)
  • The needs of those customers
  • A thing that your business can do to meet those needs
  • Organisational practices that help to achieve this goal—and to be able to carry on doing so, sustainably
  • A success criterion, such as making a profit

The Business Model Canvas

One of the most popular templates for working on a business model is the Business Model Canvas by Alexander Osterwalder. He has also a startup—the Business Model Foundry.

Business Model Secrecy

“Your business plan cannot be secret anymore. People are too smart for that, too tired of getting burned, too wary of losing their contributions when a startup dies, and too annoyed by sudden changes to terms. Communicate your business plan from the start and you'll avoid a thousand problems down the road.”
—http://powazek.com/posts/3250

Fab Lab Business Model

“commercial activities can be incubated in Fab Labs but they must not conflict with open access, they should grow beyond rather than within the lab, and they are expected to benefit the inventors, labs, and networks that contribute to their success.”
—http://fab.cba.mit.edu/about/charter

Fab Lab Iceland Models

  1. The Enabler business model
    Launch new and sustain existing Fab Labs
  2. The Education business model
    Provide education services to other Fab Labs
  3. The Incubator business model
    Help entrepreneurs create sustainable businesses out of their Fab Lab creations
  4. The Replicated / Network business model
    Provide a product or service that operates by utilizing the infrastructure, staff and expertise of many (or all) Fab Labs

Common Goal

According to a research by Peter Troxler (2010), most of the fab labs offer infrastructure to students and are mostly university funded. The common goal is to figure out a self-sustainable business model for fab labs.

Additive Manufacturing

3D Printing process for creating a three-dimensional object by adding material rather than removing it as in the case of subtractive manufacturing where material is being removed.

3D Printing

The main form of additive manufacturing today that includes different techniques of creating a real-life three-dimensional object.

Fused Deposition Modeling

With FDM three-dimensional objects are made by adding material in layers. The material can be plastic filament or metal wire, as well as concrete or plaster.

FDM History

The technology was developed by S. Scott Crump in the late 1980s and was commercialized in 1990 by Stratasys. In 2009 the patent expired, thus allowing anyone to use the technology.

FDM Popularity Today

The expiration of the patent gave birth to many kinds of open-source FDM 3D printers, as for example the RepRap project. The MakerBot is a descendant of the RepRap.

Selective Laser Sintering

SLS is an additive manufacturing technique that uses laser to sinter powdered material (usually metal) to create a three-dimensional object.

SLS Development

Developed and patented by Dr. Carl Deckard and academic adviser, Dr. Joe Beaman at the University of Texas at Austin in the mid-1980s, under sponsorship of DARPA (Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency).

SLS Patent

On January 28, 2014 (just now), the patent for the Selective Laser Sintering method expired and that means new affordable SLS 3D printers on the market soon.

OpenSLS
andreasbastian.com/opensls

Stereolithography

Optical fabrication or Resin printing, a additive manufacturing technique where light is used to solidify layers of resin in order to produce a three-dimensional object.

Patenting

The process was patented as a means of rapid prototyping in 1986 by Charles Hull, co-founder of 3D Systems, Inc., a leader in the 3D printing industry.

STL File Format

The STereoLithography file format invented by 3D Systems inc. Stores triangulated 3D surface using the three-dimensional Cartesian coordinate system.

Slicing

Generates layer information in the form of machine instructions for the 3D printer from an STL file usually.

G-Code File Format

Many variants of a file format for controlling automated machine tools. This file format applies not only to 3D printers but to CNC routers and other machines as well.
